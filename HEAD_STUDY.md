# Head-side study — fixing the head, not the backbone

## Why this document exists

Three inference-only decoders (`tiebreak`, `exec_verify`, `beam`) were built and measured against the canonical `pure` decoder on the 23-task JEPA suite. All three land at 19/23 (83%), matching `pure` and matching the upstream model card. The execution-verification decoder, which uses Unicorn as an oracle and scores trajectories by cumulative margin, **does** fire on the failing display-tier cycles but picks a different-wrong candidate (`display OK → OC` instead of `OO`). That finding is load-bearing: it means the top-k at low-margin cycles contains multiple trajectories that *halt cleanly, produce no errors, and have comparable cumulative margins* — and the oracle cannot distinguish among them because all of them are runnable programs. The information required to pick *the* correct one — that the prompt said `OK` and not `OC` — is not in the scoring signal available at inference time.

The ceiling of inference-only approaches on this checkpoint is 19/23. Additional gains require a training-time intervention. This document scopes the head-side options while honouring the README's frozen-backbone commitment.

## The frozen-backbone constraint and what it allows

The README's architectural commitment is clear: `Qwen/Qwen2.5-Coder-7B-Instruct` is frozen, bf16, untouched. This does *not* forbid:

- retraining the seven Flamingo-style adapters
- retraining the state encoder
- retraining the 2-layer `head_mlp` and the `embed_head` linear projection
- retraining the 691-row instruction codebook (`instr_table`) and its companion `instr_words` buffer
- adding *new* parameters that operate only on the existing pooled hidden state, the state tokens, or both, provided they do not require backbone gradients

The head-side study below confines itself to those degrees of freedom.

## Diagnostic: what the failing cycles actually look like

From the `exec_verify` trace on `display OK` at cycle 3, the top-4 JEPA candidates were:

| candidate | disassembly | cum_sim over 6-step lookahead |
|---|---|---|
| `0x04b00293` | `addi x5, x0, 0x4B`  (x5 ← 'K')  — wrong rd | 1.21 |
| `0x04b00793` | `addi x15, x0, 0x4B`  (x15 ← 'K')  — **correct** | 1.29 |
| `0x06500793` | `addi x15, x0, 0x65`  (x15 ← 'e') | 1.29 |
| `0x04300793` | `addi x15, x0, 0x43`  (x15 ← 'C')  — chosen | 1.31 |

Four candidates are all within 0.1 of each other on cumulative similarity. They are all same-opcode, all differing only in destination register or in the low byte of the immediate. The training corpus has programs that end in `OK`, `OC`, and `Oe` with similar frequency and no structural difference at cycle 3 — the state at cycle 3 does not carry a signal that would disambiguate among them.

The state encoder's 65 tokens at cycle 3 contain: 32 registers (0 and the pointer in x16), PC, 16 words around PC (the emitted `lui`, `addi`, `sb`), 16 words around SP. None of those encode *"I have written one byte to DISPLAY and I am about to write the second"*. That is the missing signal.

## Paths for the study, ranked by expected lift vs. complexity

### Path A — state enrichment: progress tokens (recommended first)

Add a small number of deterministic *progress tokens* to the 65-token state: e.g. a single `display_bytes_written` counter and/or a single `pc_offset_from_program_start` slot. Extend `StateEncoder` with role embeddings for the new slots. Retrain only `state_encoder` + `kv_norm` + the seven adapters + `head_mlp` + `embed_head` + `instr_table`. Backbone stays frozen.

Expected effect: cycle 3 of `display OK` now has a state token that says *"one byte written to DISPLAY"*. The adapters learn to map that token into a K/V that moves the pooled hidden state along a direction that favours `addi x15, 'K'` over `addi x15, 'C'` because the training corpus always pairs *"one byte written"* with *"second target byte"*.

Risk: if the training data's display-byte prompts are fewer than roughly 500 examples, the per-byte progress signal may be too sparse to learn reliably. Mitigation: augment the corpus with a larger synthetic set of display programs (already generated by `scripts/generate_programs.py`; sizing up costs only wall-time).

Training cost: recovering near-current eval on an L40S is described in the MODEL_CARD as ~5 h at batch 32 / 15 000 steps. A state-enrichment retrain should cost the same; the new parameters are minimal (a handful of role embeddings).

### Path B — auxiliary reranker head (non-invasive to the canonical head)

Leave the `embed_head` and `instr_table` unchanged. Add a small reranker `R(h, aux) → logits_over_topk` that takes the pooled hidden state plus an auxiliary vector (cycle count, previously-emitted opcode id, bytes-to-display counter) and outputs a reweighting of the top-k JEPA sims. Train it on the existing cycle-pool with the loss: maximise the reweighted score on the true instruction among its top-k neighbours.

At inference, the full decode becomes `pick argmax of (jepa_sim(x_i) + lambda * R(h, aux)_i)` for each codebook row `x_i`. This is architecturally a confidence-aware fusion of the JEPA signal with a learned external signal — exactly the shape recommended by the 2026 literature (kNN-TRANX, N2C2, HyRACC, NEST).

Pros: does not touch adapters, state encoder, or codebook. The canonical `pure` decoder still runs in parallel; the reranker is an *additional* output path, not a replacement.

Cons: introduces new parameters and a new training phase. The spiritual cost is comparable to `tiebreak`: the decode stage is no longer a pure function of the JEPA embedding — it is a fusion of two signals. Adding a CONFESSION entry is appropriate.

Training cost: much smaller than Path A because the rest of the network is frozen. Probably 30–60 min on a single GPU.

### Path C — contrastive head with same-opcode-aware negatives

The current codebook head trains with InfoNCE at τ = 0.07 with every other row as a negative. Same-opcode neighbours (e.g. `addi x15, 'K'` vs `addi x15, 'C'`) are negatives of equal weight with cross-opcode negatives (e.g. `addi x15, 'K'` vs `jal x0, 0`). This makes the embedding space efficient at avoiding opcode flips — consistent with the observed 100% same-opcode miss purity — but does not invest extra discrimination where it is hardest.

A modified loss weights negatives by *their closeness to the positive* in instruction-field space: same-opcode-same-rd-different-imm negatives get 4× weight; same-opcode-different-rd negatives get 2×; cross-opcode negatives get 1×. This pushes the embedding head to carve sharper boundaries exactly where the display failures live.

Pros: no new parameters, no new input channels. Just a loss change.

Cons: requires full head retraining. May trade off general accuracy for display-accuracy if over-weighted; needs ablation.

Training cost: full head retrain, same order as Path A.

### Path D — FSQ drop-in

Replace the learned 691-row codebook with Finite Scalar Quantization. The implicit codebook is a lattice in embedding space; no learnable codebook rows to collapse or over-cluster. 2025 literature says this eliminates collapse entirely.

Relevance here: low. The current checkpoint shows no codebook collapse (all 691 rows in use, every top-1 miss is same-opcode). The problem is *discrimination between close neighbours*, not *unused codebook capacity*. FSQ is noted for completeness; it is not the right tool for this failure mode.

## Priority ordering

1. **Path A (state enrichment + retrain).** Highest expected lift, cleanest alignment with the spirit of the system (state is how Reflex grounds itself; making the state more informative is the *correct* lever to pull). The only reason not to do it first is the training cost.

2. **Path B (auxiliary reranker).** If the training cost of Path A is prohibitive, Path B is the cheapest meaningful head-side intervention and the one that maps most directly onto the 2026 inference-time fusion literature. It would be explicitly confessed in `CONFESSION.md`.

3. **Path C (contrastive re-weighting).** Worth trying as an ablation alongside Path A — the two can be combined.

4. **Path D (FSQ).** Document and defer. Not the right fix for this failure mode.

## What this study does *not* attempt

- Retraining the backbone.
- Re-introducing latent recurrence. The earlier branch (`feat/latent-fidelity-testbed`) established that latent recurrence on this architecture is parasitic on pre-seq-window checkpoints and solves a problem (bit-flip fragility) that the JEPA codebook already solves at the output head.
- Adding text-generation or chain-of-thought output channels.
- Moving work into the prompt.

Any of those would constitute a different project.

## Measuring success

The current 19/23 / 83% baseline is the number to beat. A successful head-side intervention should:

1. Recover all three display-tier failures (`display OK`, `show 42`, `print hello`).
2. Not regress any of the passing tasks.
3. Not degrade per-step top-1 accuracy on the 500-cycle diagnostic below 95% (the current 96% is documented).
4. Preserve factorial-5 consistency at 10/10, 91 ops each.

The target is 22/23 or 23/23 on the 23-task suite. The `sum 1..10` failure is a prompt-interpretation issue (the model computes 1..20 instead) — it may or may not be rescued by state enrichment; a realistic target if Path A only addresses display-tier failures is **22/23 (96%)**.
